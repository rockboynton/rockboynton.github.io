UX Design

Poster Design

MSOE Hockey Home Opener

Task

My task was to individually design a poster in 2 weeks following good UX design principles such as C.R.A.P (Contrast, Repetition, Alignment, and Proximity). I also explored the use of typography, layout,and color.

Goals

My primary goals were to employ what I have learned in my course to create a visually appealing poster that was engaging and informative. I wanted to make it easy for someone to know what it is in a split second or passing glance, guide them in, and when they examined it, quickly try to promote the event and increase attendance.

Design Decisions

  • I used a block type font that really announced what the event was and stood out visually in the 3rd dimension. 
  • I chose our school’s athletic logo as the centerpiece because it is visually attractive and powerful. 
  • I placed action shots of two players flanking either side of the poster to visually show what sport the event is, as well as increase the sense of action. 
  • I used a nice backdrop shot of the MSOE Kern Center that accomplished 3 tasks simultaneously
    1. It provides the location of the event
    2. It provides the approximate time of the event
    3. It increases the sense of action with the streaking car lights
  • I blurred the background slightly to have the text stand out.
  • I highlighted a bonus incentive if the person looking at the poster was pondering whether they should go.

Results

I believe the final result shows a good use of the design principles learned in my UX course.

UX 1400 Reflection

What I learned

I am really glad I took this course to learn about User Experience research and design to see the user side of development. Before this class, I had been focusing on building my technical skills and wanted to learn more about how to design products/applications with the users in mind make very accessible.


In this course, I began learning about the psychology behind a user's perception. I learned popular design patterns and heuristics for making good design decisions as well as what to avoid.


I learned visual design principles, like C.R.A.P, to gain insight on what actually makes a user interface appealing and interesting, as well as practice in applying those using fun, interactive games. I also studied building good information architecture to have a good hierarchy of information which was especially helpful when designing my portfolio.


For the research side, I conducted user interviews for a design project, making sure to have a clear goal and focused questions to gain the insight I needed to develop a good finished product. This was my first experience doing something like this and it really helped me learn to observe carefully to pick up on some subtle things to improve in my design.


I also conducted "Make-it-work" usability research on my portfolio where I walked through my website with a potential user asking them to complete tasks that represented what I had researched to be the most common things a user would want to do. This was a great experience because it showed me that everybody has different intuition and what's easy for someone might not be easy for another.
